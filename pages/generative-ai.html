Autoencoders

Learn a compressed representation of the input by training a network to reconstruct its own input.

Key: The new (compressed) representation is learned in the bottleneck — the middle hidden/latent layer. The output layer reconstructs the original input — it does NOT produce the new representation.

Training Objective

Reconstruction Loss
\[\mathcal{L} = \|x - \hat{x}\|^2 = \sum_i (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2\]

Minimize the difference between input x and reconstructed output x̂. Forces the bottleneck to learn the most important features.

Applications

🗜️ Compression — reduce data dimensionality
🔍 Anomaly detection — high reconstruction error = anomaly
✨ Denoising — train with noisy input, clean target
🎨 Generation — sample from latent space (VAE)

Latent space interpolation: GANs map a continuous latent space to images. If you have 5 latent vectors corresponding to 5 images, you can average them and decode the average vector to produce a "blended" image.

Part 2

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

Two networks compete — a Generator trying to fool the Discriminator, a Discriminator trying to catch fakes.

The Adversarial Game

🎨 Generator (G)

Takes random noise z → generates fake data (e.g. fake images). Goal: fool the discriminator into thinking its output is real.

🔍 Discriminator (D)

Takes real or fake data → outputs probability of being real. Goal: correctly classify real vs. fake.

Minimax game: Generator improves to fool Discriminator; Discriminator improves to detect fakes. Competition drives both to improve simultaneously.

GAN Objective (minimax)
\[\min_G \max_D\; \mathbb{E}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}[\log(1-D(G(z)))]\]

Training the Generator

The generator's goal: fool the discriminator into thinking its fake images are real. So when training the generator, the target label used is "Real".

Mode collapse: Generator produces limited variety, ignoring parts of the data distribution. A well-known challenge in GAN training (NOT diffusion models).

Part 3

Diffusion Models

Add noise gradually (forward process), then learn to reverse it (denoising). More stable than GANs, but slower to generate.

How Diffusion Works

Forward Process (q)

Gradually add Gaussian noise to data over T steps. By step T, data is pure noise.

Reverse Process (p)

Train a neural network to denoise — predict and remove the noise added at each step. Start from noise → iteratively denoise → get clean image.

Diffusion vs. GAN

PropertyGANDiffusion
Generation speedFast (single forward pass)Slow (many denoising steps)
Training stabilityUnstable (mode collapse risk)More stable
Mode collapseCommon problemLess susceptible
Image qualityHighVery high (SOTA)

Quiz answer: Diffusion models are slower at generating images than GANs — they require many iterative denoising steps. GANs generate in a single forward pass.

Self-Supervised Training for Colorization

No manual labeling required. Existing color photos are trivially converted to grayscale; the original color image serves as the ground truth target. This is a form of self-supervised learning.

Part 4

Advanced CNN Applications

Image Segmentation

Output is an image of the same shape as the input, where each pixel is labeled (foreground/background or class).

Architecture: Encoder (compress) → Decoder (upsample). Skip connections preserve spatial detail (U-Net).

Used in autonomous driving

Adversarial Images

Tiny, often imperceptible pixel perturbations crafted to make a CNN misclassify with high confidence.

Demonstrates that CNNs do not closely model the human visual system — they rely on patterns humans don't notice.

Transpose Convolution

The up-sampling counterpart of convolution. Used in decoders to grow feature maps back to full image size.

Also called "deconvolution." Used in encoder-decoder architectures like U-Net and image generation models.

Object Detection

Locate and classify multiple objects in an image with bounding boxes (rectangles, not exact contours).

YOLO (You Only Look Once): single-pass object detection. Fast, open-source, state-of-the-art.
